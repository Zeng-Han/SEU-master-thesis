\begin{abstract}{大语言模型，幻觉检测，内部表征，稀疏自编码器，检索增强生成}

近年来，大语言模型在问答、生成与检索等任务中表现出强大的能力，但仍容易产生与事实不符、逻辑错误或缺乏依据的幻觉内容，影响系统的可靠性与安全性。幻觉检测任务旨在识别和检测大语言模型生成的幻觉内容，对于大语言模型在实际应用中的安全部署具有重要意义。现有方法主要分为基于输出信息的黑盒方法和基于内部表征的白盒方法。其中，内部表征是指大语言模型在生成过程中于不同层、不同位置及不同模块产生的隐藏激活状态，它包含了比最终输出文本更丰富的中间语义与推理信息，因此被认为是刻画模型生成机制与识别幻觉线索的重要依据。因此，本文聚焦于基于内部表征的幻觉检测，提出以下两类方法：

针对现有幻觉检测方法对训练数据依赖较强，导致跨数据集、跨场景泛化能力受限的问题，本文提出ATScore（Activation Tensor Score），一种基于多方向表征建模的幻觉检测方法。该方法从大语言模型生成过程中的内部表征出发，构建统一的三维激活张量表示，并分别沿层方向与词元方向进行链式表征建模，通过提取链长度、转折角度等几何统计特征，构造无需额外训练的白盒幻觉检测信号。相较于现有仅沿单一方向建模的方法，ATScore能够更充分地利用跨层与跨词元信息，挖掘内部表征演化过程中与幻觉相关的异常模式。实验结果表明，ATScore在五个公开数据集和三种评价指标上均取得了良好的检测效果，优于多种黑盒与白盒基线方法，AUROC指标相较于此前领先算法取得了至多5.61个百分点的提升，并表现出较好的跨数据集泛化能力和跨场景适用性。

在检索增强生成（Retrieval-Augmented Generation，RAG）场景下，针对现有方法对外部检索知识信号利用不足，难以有效刻画模型内部知识与外部检索知识之间冲突关系的问题，本文提出SKID（Sparse Knowledge Inconsistency Detector），一种基于检索知识冲突消解的幻觉检测方法。该方法首先利用稀疏自编码器将内部表征映射到高维空间，以获得更具语义可解释性的稀疏表征。随后，结合检索文档对输出词元进行过滤，保留未被外部知识直接支持的生成片段，以更直接地刻画并消解模型内部知识与外部检索知识之间的潜在冲突。最后，通过互信息筛选出与幻觉最相关的特征维度，训练广义加性模型实现对幻觉的有效检测。实验结果表明，SKID在四个RAG数据集和五种评价指标上均具有强竞争力，优于多类基线方法，AUROC指标相较前沿的RAG幻觉检测方法取得了3.32个百分点的领先。进一步分析表明，SKID还能够结合稀疏特征的语义解释幻觉判定依据，兼顾了检测准确性与结果可解释性。

\end{abstract}

\begin{englishabstract}{Large Language Models, Hallucination Detection, Internal Representations, Sparse Autoencoder, Retrieval-Augmented Generation}

In recent years, large language models have demonstrated powerful capabilities in tasks such as question answering, text generation, and retrieval. However, they are still prone to producing hallucinated content that is inconsistent with facts, logically flawed, or unsupported by evidence, thereby undermining the reliability and safety of such systems. Hallucination detection aims to identify hallucinated content generated by large language models and is of great significance for their safe deployment in practical applications. Existing methods can generally be divided into black-box methods based on output information and white-box methods based on internal representations. Internal representations refer to the hidden activation states produced by large language models at different layers, positions, and modules during the generation process. Compared with the final output text, these internal representations contain richer intermediate semantic and reasoning information, and are therefore regarded as an important basis for characterizing generation mechanisms and identifying hallucination-related signals. Accordingly, hallucination detection based on internal representations is investigated with two methods proposed in this thesis.

To address the problem that existing hallucination detection methods rely heavily on training data, which limits their cross-dataset and cross-scenario generalization capability, ATScore (Activation Tensor Score), a hallucination detection method based on multi-directional representation modeling, is proposed. Starting from the internal representations produced during the generation process of large language models, ATScore constructs a unified three-dimensional activation tensor representation and performs chain-like representation modeling along both the layer direction and the token direction. By extracting geometric statistical features such as chain length and turning angle, it builds a white-box hallucination detection signal without requiring additional training. Compared with existing methods that model representations along only a single direction, ATScore makes more comprehensive use of cross-layer and cross-token information and captures abnormal patterns related to hallucinations during the evolution of internal representations. Experimental results show that ATScore achieves strong detection performance on five public datasets under three evaluation metrics, outperforming a variety of black-box and white-box baselines. In particular, it improves the AUROC by up to 5.61 percentage points over the previous state-of-the-art method, while also exhibiting strong cross-dataset and cross-scenario generalization capability.

In the context of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), existing methods often fail to make sufficient use of external retrieved knowledge and thus struggle to effectively characterize the conflict between the model's internal knowledge and external retrieved knowledge. To address this issue, SKID (Sparse Knowledge Inconsistency Detector), a hallucination detection method based on retrieved knowledge conflict resolution, is proposed. First, SKID employs a sparse autoencoder to map internal representations into a high-dimensional space, thereby obtaining sparse representations with stronger semantic interpretability. It then filters output tokens based on retrieved documents, retaining only those generated fragments that are not directly supported by external knowledge, so as to more directly characterize and resolve the potential conflict between the model's internal knowledge and external retrieved knowledge. Finally, it selects the feature dimensions most relevant to hallucinations through mutual information and trains a generalized additive model to achieve effective hallucination detection. Experimental results show that SKID is highly competitive on four RAG datasets under five evaluation metrics and outperforms multiple categories of baseline methods. Specifically, it surpasses the leading RAG hallucination detection method by 3.32 percentage points in AUROC. Further analysis shows that SKID can also explain the basis for hallucination judgments by leveraging the semantics of sparse features, thus achieving both high detection accuracy and strong interpretability.

\end{englishabstract}
